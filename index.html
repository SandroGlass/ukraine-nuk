Ukraine's Nuclear Disarmament

The Story of How Ukraine Gave Up the World's Third-Largest Nuclear Arsenal

Scroll to explore the journey from nuclear power to disarmament

1991: Ukraine After Soviet Collapse

Following the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991, Ukraine inherited the third-largest nuclear arsenal in the world, with approximately 1,900 strategic nuclear warheads, 176 intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs), and 44 strategic bombers.

The map shows the extensive military and civilian nuclear facilities across Ukraine in 1991.

The Budapest Memorandum, 1994

In December 1994, Ukraine signed the Budapest Memorandum on Security Assurances, agreeing to give up its nuclear weapons in exchange for security assurances from Russia, the United States, and the United Kingdom.

The signatories promised to respect Ukraine's independence, sovereignty, and existing borders.

1994-1996: Strategic Nuclear Disarmament

Ukraine began dismantling its strategic nuclear warheads and bomber fleet. The process started with military nuclear facilities and strategic missile divisions.

By 1996, all strategic nuclear weapons had been transferred to Russia for dismantling.

Nuclear Warhead Storage and Tactical Weapons

Ukraine also gave up its tactical nuclear weapons and decommissioned nuclear warhead storage facilities across the country.

This represented a significant step toward global nuclear disarmament efforts.

Civilian Nuclear Power

Ukraine maintained its civilian nuclear power plants, which became crucial for the country's energy independence.

Nuclear power plants such as Zaporizhzhia, South Ukrainian, Rivne, and Khmelnytskyi continued to operate for peaceful energy production.

2014: Security Assurances Violated

In 2014, Russia annexed Crimea, violating the security assurances given in the Budapest Memorandum.

This action marked the beginning of territorial conflicts that would challenge Ukraine's security.

2022: Full-Scale Invasion

In February 2022, Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine, further violating the Budapest Memorandum commitments.

The invasion resulted in Russian occupation of additional Ukrainian territories in the east and south.

The Legacy of Disarmament

Ukraine's decision to give up nuclear weapons represents one of the most significant acts of nuclear disarmament in history.

The nation's experience raises important questions about nuclear deterrence and the value of security assurances in international relations.

Reflecting on Ukraine's Nuclear Disarmament

Ukraine's journey from nuclear power to disarmament showcases both the promise and challenges of international security agreements.

The current conflict has led many to reconsider the implications of Ukraine's decision to relinquish its nuclear arsenal and the reliability of security guarantees provided by global powers.
